automatic differentation and dual numbers

February 27, 2018

If you look up automatic differentation and eventually dual numbers you will get a lot of infomration but it’s hard to make sense of it all at first.

So I did what I always do i n this case, just implement the damn thing myself and see how it actually works.

data structure

I like to tackle things from a functional perspectvei so that means we need to consider our data structure first. For a dual number we’ll just gonna go with good old double (we make it more generic later) and model it as a simple struct like so:

struct Dual
{
    private readonly double Re;
    private readonly double Ep;
}

We’ll use Re for the real part and Ep for the epsilon (sometimes called dual) part of this number.

a little bit about dual numbers

Now if you go look up things about dual numbers and automatic differentation you will pretty quickly hit some scary looking math. For example, mathematicians will say that dual numbers are defined where ep * ep = 0 and that’s fine however, we don’t really need to worry about that stuff (unless we want to).

From an engineer’s point of perspective those numbers are just a smart trick to help us calculate derivatives on the fly. Basically, for every operation that we execute in the original function we execute a second operation that calculates the derivative.

a little bit about derivatives

The term derivative is from math and it basically means taking one function f and somehow calculating how fast f changes at some point. That new function that calculates the rate of change is often called f' (or f prime). Now you can actually calculate derivates from derivates as well. So you could calculcate f'' (the rate of change of the rate of change) or f''' (the rate of change of the rate of change of the rate of change) as well. You could try even higher orders but for now I think the pattern is clear.

The most important thing to remember is that a deriviate is a function g(x) that calculates the rate of change of another function at some point x.

putting stuff together

Now what we wanna do is to calculate the rate of change of some operation f(x) for some value of x. With all that discussion out of the way we’re getting closer. We also have our data structure so let’s get to work with one of the most essential of operations: adding some Dual a and some Dual b together to create a new Dual c.

factorizing numbers

March 8, 2018

In this (hopefully short) post we’ll take a quick look at factoring numbers into prime factors. My math is not that strong but basically any integer (whole) number can be factorized into prime numbers. If the number is a prime, it has only one factor, its identity and that is the number itself (i.e. factorize(5) = 5).

Some numbers have more interesting factors than other numbers. For example, if we take the number 123 we get the factors of 3 and 41 (so that 3 * 41 = 123).

Today I randomly calculated 321 and the factors 3 and 107 popped up. Now this didn’t tell me anything but for some reason I decided to start adding zeros to that number.

The number 3210 gave me 2 * 3 * 5 * 107 and from there on the 2 and 5 factors just expand.

Eventually I ended up feeding it 321000000 and it turned up with the following factors:

321_000_000 = 2 * 2 * 2 * 2 * 2 * 2 * 3 * 5 * 5 * 5 * 5 * 5 * 5 * 107

There’s quite a few interesting things about this:

  • There’s this single 3 value right in the middle (more or less)
  • We get more 2 values on the left and more 5 values on the right as we add zeros to our 321 number.
  • The number of 2’s and 5’s is equal to the number of zeros we add.
  • The final 107 term is constant no matter how many zeros we add.

Of course this is all easily explainable using math but going into that will be a slightly more involved post to write.

Another interesting thing to note is that when we have a factorization in this form we we can also write it:

321_000_000 = Math.Pow(2, 6) * 3 * Math.Pow(5, 6) * 107

If we could assume 2, 3 and 5 as constants (or given) values we could reduce it even more. We would just have to store two very small values, 6 and 107 in this case and we could backwards compute our number.
